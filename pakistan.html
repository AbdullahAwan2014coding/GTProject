History of Pakistan

Pakistan gained independence on August 14, 1947, after the partition of
British India, led by Muhammad Ali Jinnah and the All-India Muslim League. The partition
caused massive migrations and violence. After independence, Pakistan adopted a parliamentary
system, but political instability led to frequent military interventions. General Ayub Khan
seized power in 1958, initiating military rule. The 1965 war with India ended in a stalemate,
but internal tensions escalated, leading to the secession of East Pakistan and the creation
of Bangladesh in 1971 after another war with India. Zulfikar Ali Bhutto governed in the
1970s but was overthrown by General Zia-ul-Haq, who enforced Islamic laws. His rule ended
with his death in 1988, leading to a democratic period under Benazir Bhutto and Nawaz Sharif.
Another military coup in 1999 brought General Pervez Musharraf to power until democracy
was restored in 2008. In 2018, Imran Khan became Prime Minister but was removed in 2022
amid political turmoil. Pakistan's history reflects resilience amid challenges, from
political instability to economic struggles. Its future depends on strengthening democracy,
economic reforms, and social development. With a young population and strategic importance,
Pakistan has the potential for growth. Stability, good governance, and innovation will be key
to shaping a prosperous and sustainable future.



Brief Description and Geography

Pakistan is a small country in South Asia. It is bordered by Afghanistan, China, India, and
Iran. The geography of Pakistan is diverse with the Thar Desert in the east and the Hindu Kush
and Pamir mountain ranges in the north. The main physical features of Pakistan include the northern
mountains (Himalayas, Karakoram, Hindu Kush), western mountains (Sulaiman, Kirthar), fertile plains and
flowing rivers of Punjab and Sindh, coastal areas along the Arabian Sea, and the Thar Desert. It is the
fifth-most populous country, with a population of over 241.5 million. It also has the second-largest
Muslim population as of 2023. Islamabad is the nation's capital.



Pakistan's Provinces

  • 1. Balochistan

    Capital:

    Quetta

    Nickname:

    The Land of Minerals

    Background:

    The largest province by area but sparsely populated, Balochistan is rich in natural resources, including minerals and gas. It has a rugged terrain with deserts, mountains, and coastline.

    Famous for:

    Gwadar Port (CPEC project), Ziarat (Quaid-e-Azam’s Residency), Hingol National Park, deep-sea fishing, Sajji (roasted lamb dish), and traditional Balochi embroidery.

  • 2. Khyber Pakhtunkhwa (KPK)

    Capital:

    Peshawar

    Nickname:

    The Land of the Brave

    Background:

    Known for its warrior culture, KPK shares a deep connection with Afghan and Pashtun history. The region is known for its breathtaking landscapes and a history of resistance against invaders.

    Famous for:

    Peshawar’s Qissa Khwani Bazaar, Khyber Pass, Swat Valley (mini-Switzerland), Malam Jabba ski resort, Chapli Kabab, and Pashto music.

  • 3. Punjab

    Capital:

    Lahore

    Nickname:

    The Land of Five Rivers

    Background:

    The most populous and economically developed province, Punjab is the cultural and political heart of Pakistan. The province is known for its fertile land and rich history.

    Famous for:

    Lahore’s historic sites (Badshahi Mosque, Lahore Fort), food street, Sufi shrines, agriculture (wheat, rice, sugarcane), and Punjab’s vibrant culture.

  • 4. Sindh

    Capital:

    Karachi

    Nickname:

    The Gateway of Islam

    Background:

    Sindh has a strong historical significance, being home to the Indus Valley Civilization (Mohenjo-Daro) and the arrival point of Islam in South Asia. Karachi, its capital, is Pakistan’s largest city and financial hub.

    Famous for:

    Karachi’s seaport, Mohenjo-Daro ruins, Sindhi Ajrak and Topi, Sufi shrines (Shah Abdul Latif Bhittai, Lal Shahbaz Qalandar), Sindhi cuisine, and mangoes.

Religon and Customs

The majority of Pakistan follows the religon islam and
speakes the language urdu. Some behavior customs are:

  • Wearing modest clothing that doesn't draw attention to one's figure
  • Standing when someone enters a room
  • Not sitting with one's legs outstretched
  • Not laughing loudly in public
  • Not interacting with children, regardless of age
  • Not bringing alcohol as a gift. See more about this in islam

Some cutural values are:

  • Collectivist culture, where people are loyal to those
    in their inner circle
  • Social connections are important for daily life
  • The concept of wasta, which is relationship forming
  • Child naming ceremonies are held 40 days after birth
  • Ramadan is a holy month for Muslims, where they fast, pray, and reflect

Food and Drinks of Pakistan

Some food and drinks pakistan are famous for are:

  • Nihari. Known as the "national dish of Pakistan"
  • Chicken Lahori. Has always been one of the most popular curries in Pakistan
  • Pakoras. Pakoras are best eaten while still hot
  • Kebabs. There are many different kinds of kebabs in Pakistani culture, including the shami kebab
  • Gol gappa. Gol gappa is a popular streetfood
  • Biryani. A popular rice dish made with a variety of meats, vegetables, and spices, often served with yogurt or
    raita
  • Chicken karahi.A dish of chicken cooked in a thick, spiced tomato gravy with ginger-garlic and cardamom, often
    served with roti or naan
  • Sajji.A dish of salt-marinated, skewered, and roasted whole chicken or lamb, often stuffed with potatoes or rice,
    and traditionally roasted over an open fire
  • Bun kebab.A street food consisting of a bun filled with a shami kebab patty, omelet, thinly sliced vegetables, and
    chutneys
  • Paratha. A Punjabi bread made with flour, oil, and sometimes ghee, often served with daal or spicy curries
  • Lassi. A traditional drink made from yogurt, water, and spices, often with the addition of fruit
  • Roghni naan. A type of Pakistani bread that is much softer and fluffier than other types of bread
  • Chapli kebab. A traditional dish from the Peshawar region of Pakistan, often served with sauces, chutney, and yogurt
    alongside with naan or bread
  • Haleem. A stew made with meat and lentils, spiced with coriander, fresh ginger, and chilli peppers, and garnished with
    fried onions and lemon
  • Tandoori chicken. A national food of Pakistan, made by marinating chicken in a mixture of yogurt, lemon juice,
    and spices, and traditionally cooked in a tandoor oven
